The one idea under all of it: a language model isn't a black box of magic — it's made of small reusable circuits you can find, watch, and switch off. Experiment 01 finds one. Everything else pokes at it, proves it's real, watches it being born, and then asks the same questions about safety.
exp 01 the setup

go deeper — why random tokens?

Random tokens have no pattern to learn from grammar or facts. "Paris is the capital of ___" you can finish from knowledge. But x7 q2 ∆ 9k … repeated? The only signal is the repetition itself. So if the model nails copy 2, that ability can't be memorized trivia — it must be in-context learning: reading the prompt and copying. That's why this stimulus is the classic probe for the induction circuit.

In the code: torch.manual_seed(0) draws the random tokens, a BOS token is prepended, and the sequence is SEQ=50 long, twice.

This is in-context learning you can see. Copy 1 stays high (nothing to copy yet). Copy 2 plunges — the model found the pattern in its own prompt. The very first copy-2 token is still a guess (nothing precedes it to copy), which is why the cliff starts one token in.
go deeper — what does "ablate" actually do?

A handful of attention heads do the copying. Ablate = reach in during the forward pass and delete just those heads' output (set it to zero), leaving everything else untouched. If the cliff refills, those heads caused the behaviour — this is a causal test, not a correlation. On gpt2 the copy-2 surprise jumps 36× when you do this.

In the code: a forward hook on blocks.{L}.attn.hook_z zeroes the chosen heads' slice. Chart is induction_3_ablation_*.png.

exp 01 the mechanism

What an induction head actually does

An attention head lets each token "look back" at earlier tokens. An induction head has one specific habit: find where this token appeared before, then look at whatever came right after it. Hover the boards to see it.

hover any token in copy 2 → watch the head point back
Hover a highlighted token to see the induction move: “I've seen you before — so I bet what came next then comes next now.”
go deeper — so what's the "induction score" in the heatmaps?

Every head gets one number: how strongly its attention lands on that look-back-and-shift diagonal, averaged over the sequence. High score = "this head is an induction head." Plot the score for every (layer, head) and you get induction_2_heatmap_*.png — the dots are the heads above threshold (0.3). gpt2 has a couple; Qwen3.5-4B stacks 34 of them.

Formally it's the attention on the stripe at offset -(SEQ-1) — from each token back to the one that followed its previous occurrence.

exp 02 the control

"But maybe any heads matter?"

Fair challenge. So: ablate the same number of randomly chosen heads instead, 10 times. If random ablations barely hurt while the induction heads devastate — the damage is specific to the circuit, not to losing some heads.

pick a model — how far above "random damage" do the induction heads sit?
go deeper — what's a "σ" (sigma)?

σ (standard deviation) measures the spread of the 10 random trials. Saying induction ablation is "102σ above the random mean" (Pythia-1.4B) means: on the bell curve of random damage, the induction result isn't just a bit worse — it's absurdly, off-the-chart worse. Astronomically unlikely to be a coincidence. That's how you turn "it looks important" into a number.

exp 06 · 13 the honest way to break things

Three ways to switch a head off

"Delete its output" sounds simple, but what do you replace it with? Zeros? Its average? A value from a control run? Each is more faithful than the last — and your experiments show the choice changes the answer.

same circuit, three methods → how much damage? (copy-2 surprise ×, your exp-13 numbers)
go deeper — the "faithfulness ladder"

Zero is off-distribution — the model never saw an all-zero activation in training, so it overreacts (overstates damage ~1.5× on gpt2/Pythia). Mean keeps a realistic size but strips the input-specific info. Patch substitutes a genuine activation from a matched "corrupt" run (same copy 1, fresh non-repeating copy 2) — zero distribution shock, the gold standard. The big finding: patch and zero agree on which backup heads exist, but the damage ranking is model-dependent — there's no universal "zero > patch > mean".

exp 03 · 04 composition

The circuit has two halves

The induction head can only copy "what came after last time" if some earlier head already tagged each token with who came before it. That upstream helper is the previous-token head. Cut it, and the induction head goes blind — without touching the induction head at all.

Composition: the behaviour isn't in one head — it's two heads talking across layers. Break the conversation upstream and the downstream head has nothing to say.
go deeper — the "just-in-time shift register" (exp 04)

In the hybrid Qwen3.5 models there were no previous-token heads in the normal attention layers — the upstream signal was hiding in the model's linear-attention layers, which act like local shift-registers feeding the induction layers just in time. Experiment 04 (layer knockout) traced exactly which layers by knocking out one at a time and watching the induction score dip. Same detective move, different suspect.

exp 05 · 13 redundancy · self-repair

The Hydra: cut a head, a backup wakes up

Ablate the main induction heads and re-score every remaining head inside the broken model. In some models, heads that looked useless suddenly step up to do the job — backups that were invisible until needed. Cut one head, two grow back.

round-by-round: ablate → look for new volunteers → repeat
go deeper — is the Hydra real, or a zeroing artifact? (exp 13)

Great skeptical question — and you tested it. If backups only "appear" because zero-ablation shocks the model off-distribution, they'd vanish under faithful patching. They didn't: on Qwen3-1.7B, patch recruits the same backups as zero (L21H9, L18H4, L8H5). So the self-repair is a genuine property of the network, not a measurement artifact. The other models recruit nobody — their robustness (where it exists) is spread thin below any threshold, not concentrated in hidden backups.

exp 12 you trained these · real data

Grow one yourself — the data decides

You trained a tiny model from scratch, three times, changing only the data mix. Story-only data never grows the circuit. Add the right pressure and it ignites in a single 8-million-token window. The recipe matters, not just the size.

induction score over training — click a recipe to toggle it
The whole circuit was two heads. Your ignited model's induction lived in exactly L3H0 + L3H1, fed by prev-token heads L2H1 / L2H7 one layer up. Zero just that pair → copy-2 surprise rockets from 0.72 back to 10.95. Textbook-minimal, and you built it.
go deeper — why doesn't TinyStories alone work?

Interesting split: the upstream half (prev-token heads) forms for free on almost any text — it reached 0.57 even on stories alone. But composition — wiring that upstream signal into a copying circuit — only happens when the data rewards copying. The synthetic repeated-block rows are that reward: the only way to predict them is to copy. 85% real text + 15% synthetic was enough to ignite; 30% wikitext with no synthetic stalled at 0.065 after 445M tokens. The circuit needs a reason to exist.

exp 08 the safety pivot

Same tools, new question: refusal

A chat model's decision to refuse turns out to live along a single direction in its activations. Slide it: remove the direction and a harmful prompt gets answered; add it and a harmless prompt gets refused. A clean double-dissociation.

Necessary and sufficient. Remove the direction → refusal collapses (it was needed). Add it → refusal appears on safe prompts (it's enough on its own). The same "find it / break it / prove it" recipe from induction heads, pointed at a safety behaviour.
go deeper — how do you "find a direction"?

Run a batch of harmful prompts and a batch of harmless ones. Average each group's residual-stream activation at the last token, and subtract. That difference vector is the refusal direction (pick the layer where the two groups separate best — here layer 21 of Qwen2.5-1.5B). Then "remove it" = project it out of every layer; "add it" = add it in. Directions are the feature-level cousin of heads — and they're exactly where mech-interp is heading next (sparse autoencoders find thousands of them).

go deeper — why this matters for alignment

"We unlearned it" is a safety claim, and this shows a safety claim can be an artifact of the metric. NPO looks fully forgotten if you read the model's greedy answer — but the ground-truth answer's probability is only suppressed, and one intervention (ablating the refusal direction) lifts it 4× back above the floor. GradDiff genuinely forgot; RMU barely forgot on Q&A at all. Same word — "unlearned" — three different realities. Building the tools and evals that catch this is a real contribution to safe, trustworthy AI. It's the direction we talked about tonight.
